# Assumption ledger — test the "obvious" beliefs first

Every idea rests on market assumptions the owner didn't think to question. The riskiest ones hide behind the word *obviously*. Obviousness is a bias signal, not evidence — the belief that feels most self-evident is often the one that, if wrong, kills the idea. Surface them, rank them, and test the top one or two *before* building.

This is prior to the line-8 hypothesis. The hypothesis says "we'll know it's true if…"; the ledger decides *which* belief the hypothesis should attack. A strong hypothesis targets the riskiest assumption, not a safe one.

## How to build the ledger

1. **Enumerate the leap-of-faith assumptions** — the market beliefs that must be true for the idea to work. Prompt for the unstated ones: who the customer is, that the pain is real and urgent, that they'll pay, that you can reach them affordably, that a named channel will actually distribute for you.
2. **Flag the "obvious" ones.** If the owner states a belief as self-evident ("founders are obviously desperate for this", "VCs will obviously share it"), mark it — those get tested first, not last.
3. **Rank by risk × load-bearing.** Two axes: how *uncertain* is it, and how much does the idea *collapse* if it's false? Top-right (uncertain + load-bearing) is where validation goes first.
4. **Name the test.** For each top assumption, the cheapest test that could prove it false — usually New Venture Framework problem validation (survey/interviews before the solution), a channel probe (can you actually get one warm intro from the assumed channel?), or a willingness-to-pay probe.

## Worked example — AI-Powered Feedback Platform

- "Funded founders are desperate for user insight" — *feels obvious* → test via problem-validation interviews before building.
- "Investors will distribute it across their portfolio" — **most load-bearing + unconfirmed** → this is why Day-120 traction was held at 1. Test with one real intro before betting the acquisition plan on it.
- "Founders will pay $49–99 on a card without approval" — moderate risk → test in the pilot conversion step.

## Output

Add a **"Test first"** line to the verdict block naming the one or two riskiest assumptions, and flag any that were stated as obvious. An idea whose riskiest assumption is untested is a REFINE, however high the rest of the card scores — you don't yet know if the market is real.
