# The fit instrument — Sean Ellis test

The 999 framework lists "PMF signal" as a 60- and 90-day review axis but never defines how to measure it. This is that instrument. It turns "PMF signal" from a vibe into a number Dmitry can gate on.

## The test

Survey users: **"How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?"**
- Very disappointed
- Somewhat disappointed
- Not disappointed
- N/A — I no longer use it

**Benchmark: ≥ 40% "very disappointed" ≈ you likely have product-market fit.** Below it, you're refining, not scaling.

## How to run it well

- Only survey users who have **reached core value** (e.g., used it ≥ 2×, hit the core workflow). Surveying tourists gives noise — so is running it on too few or too-fresh users.
- Segment the **"very disappointed"** cohort — that group is your **High-Expectation Customer (HXC)**. Build for them, not the median user.
- Ask the fanatics: **"What's the main benefit you get?"** Ask the near-miss "somewhat disappointed" group: **"What one change would make this a must-have?"**
- Don't scale spend before you clear ~40% — you'll pour the $5k marketing cap into a leaky bucket.

## Wiring to the 999 clock

- **Pre-build:** don't survey a product that doesn't exist. Run the New Venture Validation Framework — problem validation (Phase 3) before solution validation (Phase 4). Look for weekly pain, ≥ 3.5/5 severity, dissatisfaction with today's workaround, and a commitment action (sign-up/waitlist/deposit). Do **not** mention the solution during problem validation.
- **Live, few users:** measure activation and week-2 retention; log who reached core value so you have an eligible survey pool later. Not enough activated users yet → the survey number won't mean anything.
- **60- & 90-day reviews:** run the survey on activated users; report the "very disappointed" percentage as the PMF axis, alongside CAC, budget, and team. This is the concrete evidence the review is supposed to weigh.
- **Retention is the throughline:** the 120-day user count can be hit with a burst and still have no fit. The Sean Ellis number and retention curve are what separate "shipped" from "fits."

## Early-adopter PMF is not the finish line — the roadmap beyond 180 days

The 40% you prove by day 180 is **early-adopter PMF**. Early adopters are forgiving and self-select for your wedge; clearing 40% *with them* is necessary, but it is not proof the product scales. The venture roadmap runs past the 999 clock:

1. **MVP** (≤ day 30).
2. **Early-adopter PMF** (through ~day 180) — the Sean Ellis 40% among activated early users; cash-flow positive; the spin-off thresholds.
3. **Scaling** — attempted growth through sales and marketing.
4. **Re-evaluation** — re-test PMF *beyond early adopters* (a broader, less forgiving cohort — the chasm), re-examine the scaling assumptions (do CAC and channels hold at volume?), refine the product, accelerate iteration.

**Two consequences for a 1st-Pass read:**

- **Don't conflate the two PMFs.** Flag any plan that assumes early-adopter love will automatically carry to the mainstream. Re-running the Sean Ellis test on the broader cohort at the scaling stage is the honest check.
- **Sales & marketing belong to *scaling*, not to reaching profitability.** Sales *effort as the path to profit* is a red flag that fights the software-first gate (see `red-flag-shapes.md`). Sales & marketing to *scale a product that already has early-adopter PMF and is cash-flow-positive* is a legitimate later-stage lever. Same activity, different stage, opposite meaning.

This stage-4 work sits beyond the documented 180-day clock and beyond spin-off — it is growth-stage, not 1st-Pass. A pre-proposal check does **not** score scaling readiness; it only sets the expectation and flags conflation.
